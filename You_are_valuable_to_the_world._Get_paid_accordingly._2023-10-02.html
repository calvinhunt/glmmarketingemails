In my ERISA long-term disability practice for doctors, I consult with and represent physicians and other health care providers who have been denied long-term disability benefits under their employer’s group disability insurance policy.  They are not working, usually have something pretty serious going on, and are reliant on the promised benefits.

 

Importantly, they are high wage earners.

 

We do appeals and consults all over the country (the ERISA regulations allow it) and team up with local counsel when we need to litigate. (Recently argued an interesting case in the Third Circuit Court of Appeals in Philadelphia. The client worked for a coal company in Southwest Virginia, we litigated in the District Court in Pittsburgh and the travel for the oral arguments in the Court of Appeals was the shortest travel in the case. You can listen to my argument to the panel at a recent episode of the Renegade Lawyer Podcast.)

 

We have some good, unique, referral sources for our claimants who are doctors and when they reach out for a consult, we charge them $1,995 for the consult, which usually takes about 90 minutes of my time total. (My team does other work to prepare us for the consult.)

 

Recently, I was contacted by a young lawyer from out of state who wanted to know how on earth I was getting almost $2,000 for a consult that didn't take much of my time. She was in the same practice area and wasn't making that much money at it, despite working long hours and despite being in thalmost every weekend.

 

What I told her (don’t know if she will “hear” me) is that she has a great deal of expertise that her clients need and that very few attorneys across the country have chosen to develop. (Really, there are maybe 150 in the U.S. who do this work on a consistent basis and only 2 or 3 others near me that I compete with.) I explained that these physician clients had large policies and that if they were truly disabled and followed the steps I set out for them, what they were paying me was 'nothing' compared to the benefit.

 

On the other hand, if these doctors made a mistake with their claim, that mistake could cost them millions, depending on how old they are and how much they were making.

 

See, most lawyers end up under-charging for their services because they do not see value in themselves. They are fearful of getting paid what they are worth.

 

Know what this leads too? 

 

A stressful life. 

 

The lack of money is the root cause of lawyer stress.

  • Not enough money to hire better help; 
  • Not enough money to invest in technology that would improve the client experience; 
  • Not enough money to be able to set aside the time necessary to get really good at a very niched practice where there is still almost no competition; and yes,
  • Not enough money to attend conferences where you can be in a room with lawyers truly value their own worth, talk freely and without 'embarrassment' about how good their lives are; and
  • Not enough money to get out of the office to develop those human relationships that will provide a steady flow of perfect clients to you.

 

I have positioned myself as top guru in the region in these cases. I’m actually really good at them and I have a well-trained team that is really good at them. We DO have a great success rate. My clients are getting a good deal when they get me on their team.

 

If you don’t feel that your clients are getting something special when they hire you and that, no matter what your fees, they are getting a remarkable bargain vis-à-vis the problem they find themselves in, then you are always going to struggle with setting and collecting fees. This isn’t about being greedy. This is about being valuable to the world. 

 

Never be shy about what you make in this business. The public needs you.
